The product is intelligent video analytics and integrated response system to address the surveillance needs of any organization.  It could detect intrusion, loitering, crowd gathering, objects dropped/removed and could monitor vehicles for threats of any type allowing organizations to deal with any issues promptly.

Stakeholder Interview/Discussion
Learnings
The UX Research and Design Process started with Stakeholders discussions. Stakeholder was anyone involved in bringing the features to fruition. In this scenario, this included director level executives as well as developers.
The discussions with stakeholders allowed me to be on the same page as them from the beginning. I understood the business goals, existing/brainstormed feature designs, the target market, the target users, the underlying technology as well as the competition. Overall, those discussions gave me a better grasp of how to proceed with the UX Process.
Some of the discussion points were:

•    What was the product?
•    Who was the product for?
•    What was the market for the product?
•    When was the version to be released?
•    What technology-related decisions had been made?
•    What constraints or worries existed regarding the product?

It is an intelligent video analytics and integrated response system to address the surveillance needs of an organization. It would generate alerts related to intrusion detection, vehicle monitoring,  loitering and crowd detection, objects dropped or removed detection and vehicle monitoring, etc.; a few cases for which this product would be used.

Learnings
I gathered information about the competition to understand the potential in the market. Several direct and indirect competitors were analysed. It gave me some valuable insights as well as the feature gap with the competitor products.

The process was based off of Jakob Nielsen's standards and the law of diminishing returns - choosing to complete three individual moderated interviews.  All interviews were recorded in the form of notes with participant consent.
I gained valuable insights from the interview/survey results which gave me a deeper understanding of potential users, their biases, motivations, and understanding of technology. All of the potential users felt they needed to be more adept with technology moving forward to be an invaluable asset to their organization.

Understanding the User
ParaSurveillance was going to be used by users with varied educational background – from high school pass outs with limited exposure to technology to graduate degree holders.

So, goal with the project was simple – design the product keeping the UX minimalistic.  

My high-level goals were to:

• Make it simple, fast, and not intimidating, for all users, everywhere.
• Create the platform with a clear and simple focus.
• Utilize the emotions associated with the surveillance process to define the app style.

User Flow
Learnings
I created the user-flow at a fairly early stage of the project. Some may even argue that it was more of a task-flow at this stage rather than the user-flow, but I like to call it that.

Creating the user-flow early on gave me a high-level view of the project coupled with added clarity that comes with creating the user-flow.  This afforded me a better grip through-out the designing process. Simultaneously, I consistently iterated on the user-flow as the project progressed based on any changes in the problem statement or definition.
The initial versions of the user-flow were too lean.  Additional screens were added to account for Human Interface Guidelines.  Specifically, the concept of always providing a clear path was getting overshadowed by focusing on limited interactions. I decided to give a clear path with additional interactions to help the user navigate with more ease.

What I learned from this project?
Lessons Learned
Developing ParaSurveillance was both a professional as well as a personal journey in which I needed to be mindful that I wasn’t creating the product for myself. I consistently went back to the persona to remove myself from the process.

The insights and feedback of the stakeholders and the team were paramount in giving me clarity about the project that resulted in a better, still developing product. After all, as Steve Jobs said:

“Great things in business are never done by one person; they're done by a team of people.”

• Always remember the goals of each design phase and don’t get caught up in the details too early or too much.
• Never underestimate design conventions. Creating something new or different might seem exciting, especially from a designer's point of view, but conventions are there because they have stood more than just the test of time.
• User needs and business goals always go hand in hand. I had to dive deeply into understanding the business perspective and figure out how to make ParaSurveillance user-centred.
• When in doubt or find yourself stuck, take a step back to take a bird- eye view. The Bird-eye view of the project often let one see the issues more clearly.
